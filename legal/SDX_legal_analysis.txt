# Legal Master Summary: Nicopod Gamification App (Germany)
Laws, Full Compliance Checklist & Gamification Feature Assessment
Prepared for: Tarayan Norman and developers of the SDX® Project | Jurisdiction: Germany / EU |
Target Launch: June/July 2026
---
Part 1: All Legally Relevant Laws and Regulations
A. EU-Level Law (Directly Applicable)
1. General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) — Regulation (EU) 2016/679
Your single most important legal framework. Governs all personal data collected from users (ratings,
consumption patterns, location, age). Key provisions:
● Recital 26: Truly anonymised data falls outside GDPR scope — your anonymisation
architecture must meet this standard
● Article 5: Core principles — lawfulness, purpose limitation, data minimisation, accuracy,
storage limitation, integrity, accountability
● Article 9: Special category data (health-adjacent nicotine consumption data) — requires
explicit consent and a specific legal basis
● Article 13: Transparency — users must be informed at collection time of all processing
purposes, including data sale
● Article 25: Privacy by design and by default — your k-anonymity and l-diversity architecture
directly satisfies this
● Article 28: Data Processing Agreements — mandatory for every B2B data buyer (NTB etc.)
● Article 35: Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) — mandatory before launch given
health-adjacent data at scale
2. Digital Services Act (DSA) — Regulation (EU) 2022/2065
Applies to your web app and future app store version as an information society service. Key
obligations:
● Article 28: Requires appropriate and proportionate measures to ensure high-level privacy,
safety, and security for minors on any platform accessible to them — age assurance is
explicitly required
● Article 35: Risk mitigation measures for systemic risks, including targeted measures to protect
minors
● Requires transparency in commercial communications (your Snuzone affiliate link must be
clearly labelled as commercial)
3. Commission Guidelines on Minors Online — C/2025/5519
Issued pursuant to DSA Article 28(4). Explicitly identifies nicotine products as a category requiring
age verification (not just self-declaration) for online platforms. Age assurance measures are
considered appropriate and proportionate for platforms involving nicotine.
4. Tobacco Advertising Directive — Directive 2003/33/EC
Prohibits advertising of tobacco products in information society services. Directly relevant caveat:
tobacco-free nicotine pouches are NOT tobacco products under this Directive (which covers products
"consisting, even partly, of tobacco"). Your app is outside the direct scope of this Directive. However,
it signals the regulatory intent and direction.
5. Tobacco Products Directive (TPD) — Directive 2014/40/EU
Regulates tobacco and related products. Tobacco-free nicopods fall outside its scope as they contain
no tobacco. This is your key regulatory gap — and it works in your favour for now. The European
Commission has acknowledged this gap explicitly in its TPD review.
6. WHO Framework Convention on Tobacco Control (FCTC)
The EU and Germany are parties. Article 13 calls for comprehensive bans on tobacco and nicotine
product advertising, promotion, and sponsorship, explicitly including apps, games, and interactive
digital media. Not yet hard law for your specific product category, but it is the clear direction of future
regulation. Design your app to be defensible against this trajectory.
---
B. German National Law (Consult a German Lawyer for Full Text)
7. Tabakerzeugnisgesetz (TabakerzG)
German transposition of the TPD. Follows the EU definition of tobacco products — tobacco-free
nicopods are currently outside its direct scope. Verify with a German lawyer whether any 2024/2025
amendments extend coverage to nicotine pouches specifically.
8. Jugendschutzgesetz (JuSchG) — Youth Protection Act
The 2021 reform extended obligations to digital services. Requires technically robust age verification
for digital services involving age-restricted products or content. A Google login or checkbox is not
sufficient. You must implement a verified age assurance system.
9. Gesetz gegen den unlauteren Wettbewerb (UWG) — Unfair Competition Act
Governs commercial communications and marketing. Your affiliate/referral link to Snuzone and any
product recommendations must comply with UWG transparency requirements. Undisclosed
commercial relationships are prohibited.
10. Heilmittelwerbegesetz (HWG) — Therapeutic Products Advertising Act
Potentially relevant to your harm reduction features (MouTrack gum advice, nicotine consumption
warnings). If these features make health-related claims, they may trigger HWG obligations. Get
specific legal advice on the wording of all harm reduction messaging.
11. Bundesdatenschutzgesetz (BDSG) — Federal Data Protection Act
German implementation of GDPR. Adds national-level requirements, including specific rules on
employee data and certain processing activities. Appoint a Data Protection Officer (DPO) if your
processing of health-adjacent data is large-scale — likely required here.

Part 2: Full Compliance Checklist
Section 1: Age Verification (CRITICAL — Pre-Launch)
● [ ] Implement technically robust age verification — not a Google login, not a checkbox.
Use a recognised German age verification service (e.g., Verimi, IDnow, or a SCHUFA-based
check)
● [ ] Gate the entire app behind age verification — no content accessible before verification
● [ ] Log verification events for accountability purposes
● [ ] App store compliance — submit app under 17+/18+ age rating on both Apple App Store
and Google Play; review both platforms' nicotine content policies before submission
---
Section 2: GDPR and Data Architecture (CRITICAL — Pre-Launch)
● [ ] Complete a formal DPIA (Data Protection Impact Assessment) before launch —
document it, store it, make it available to the supervisory authority (BfD / LfD) on request
● [ ] Appoint a Data Protection Officer (DPO) — required for large-scale processing of
health-adjacent data
● [ ] Implement k-anonymity (k=10) — minimum 10 reviewers per product per region before
any data is released to buyers. Already designed — confirm technical implementation
● [ ] Implement l-diversity on sensitive rating dimensions (nicotine strength, bite) — each
anonymised group must contain at least l meaningfully different values to prevent
homogeneity attacks
● [ ] Regional generalisation — map user locations to the nearest major region (Munich,
Hannover, etc.) before any data is stored or processed. Already designed — confirm this
happens at input, not at output
● [ ] Age bracket generalisation — store age groups (18-20, 21-25, etc.), never exact ages
● [ ] AI summarisation of all written reviews — no raw text from individual users ever leaves
your system or enters the anonymised dataset
● [ ] Conduct periodic re-identification risk assessments — anonymisation is not a one-time
act; review as technology evolves
● [ ] Register as data controller with the relevant German supervisory authority
---
Section 3: Consent Architecture (CRITICAL — Pre-Launch)
Implement three separate, granular consent flows — never bundle them:
● [ ] Consent 1 — General App Use: Collection of ratings, product scans, consumption data
for app functionality and personalised recommendations
● [ ] Consent 2 — Anonymised Data Sale: Explicit disclosure that anonymised, aggregated,
product-scoped insights are sold to manufacturers. Must state: (a) data is anonymised, (b)
manufacturers only see their own products, (c) no individual data is ever shared. This is a
transparency obligation under Article 13 GDPR — disclose at collection time
● [ ] Consent 3 — MouTrack Opt-In: Separate, explicit consent for mouth-side tracking. Must
state: (a) data is used only for the individual user's harm reduction, (b) data is never sold or
shared, (c) user can withdraw at any time with immediate deletion
● [ ] Consent withdrawal mechanism — users must be able to withdraw any consent at any
time, with immediate effect
● [ ] Consent logging — store timestamped records of all consent events for accountability
---
Section 4: Privacy Policy and Legal Documentation (Pre-Launch)
● [ ] Privacy policy — plain language, covering: what data is collected, why, how long it is
kept, who it is sold to (anonymised, product-scoped), user rights (access, deletion, portability,
objection)
● [ ] Terms of Service — including 18+ requirement, prohibited uses, and disclaimer that the
app is not a medical device and harm reduction features are informational only
● [ ] Data Processing Agreements (DPAs) with every B2B data buyer — must include:
● Explicit prohibition on re-identification attempts
● Prohibition on combining the licensed dataset with any other dataset
● Scope limitation to licensed products only (e.g., NTB can only access Cuba Black if that is the
licensed product)
● Audit rights for you as controller
● Data deletion obligations at end of license period
● [ ] Affiliate/referral link disclosure — the Snuzone link must be clearly labelled as a
commercial/affiliate link in compliance with UWG
---
Section 5: Harm Reduction Features (HWG Review)
● [ ] Legal review of all harm reduction messaging under HWG — specifically: MouTrack
gum advice, nicotine consumption warnings, and any language suggesting health benefits
● [ ] Add a clear disclaimer that the app is not a medical device, harm reduction features are
informational only, and users should consult a healthcare professional for medical advice
● [ ] Nicotine consumption warnings — frame as informational tracking, not medical advice
---
Section 6: Commercial Features (UWG / DSA)
● [ ] Label the Snuzone affiliate link as a commercial/sponsored link — undisclosed
commercial relationships violate UWG
● [ ] Ensure the Discover feature (product recommendations) is based on user preference data,
not paid placement — if manufacturers can pay for placement in Discover, this must be
disclosed as advertising
● [ ] DSA transparency — if your app qualifies as an online platform under DSA, commercial
communications must be clearly identifiable as such
---
Section 7: App Store and Technical
● [ ] Apple App Store: Submit under 17+ age rating; review Apple's guidelines on nicotine/
tobacco content (Section 1.4 of App Store Review Guidelines)
● [ ] Google Play: Submit under "Adults Only" or equivalent; review Google Play's Dangerous
Products policy on nicotine
● [ ] Secure data storage — encrypt all personal data at rest and in transit (GDPR Article 32)
● [ ] Data breach response plan — document your procedure for notifying the supervisory
authority within 72 hours of a breach (GDPR Article 33)

Part 3: Gamification Features — Can You Keep Them?
Rarities (Common to Legendary)
Status: YES — Keep it.
Rarity tiers assigned to nicopod products are a product classification and engagement mechanic. They
do not constitute advertising of a tobacco product (nicopods are outside the TPD scope). They do not
make health claims. They are a standard gamification feature comparable to trading card games or
collectible apps.
One caveat: Rarity must not be tied to purchasing volume or frequency in a way that incentivises
overconsumption. If "Legendary" status requires scanning 100 pouches of a product, this could be
characterised as a consumption-incentivising mechanic that conflicts with your harm reduction
positioning and potentially with future nicotine product regulation. Tie rarity to discovery (scanning
new products) rather than quantity consumed.
---
The Dex (Product Catalogue, Greyed Out Until Scanned)
Status: YES — Keep it.
A product catalogue that unlocks through user discovery is a standard app mechanic. It does not
advertise products — it catalogues them based on user interaction. The greyed-out display of
unscanned products does not constitute promotion of those products.
One caveat: The Dex displays product packaging visuals. Ensure that the visual display of packaging
is neutral and informational (showing what the product looks like) rather than promotional (no
marketing copy, no brand slogans, no health claims). This keeps it clearly on the catalogue side of the
advertising line.
---
Discover Feature (Recommendations for Similar Products)
Status: YES — Keep it, with one structural condition.
Recommending products similar to ones the user already consumes is a standard recommendation
engine mechanic (comparable to Spotify's "You might also like"). It is based on user preference data,
not paid placement, and serves the user's existing consumption patterns.
The structural condition: If manufacturers can pay to appear in Discover recommendations (i.e.,
paid placement), this becomes advertising and must be:
1. 2. Clearly labelled as "Sponsored" or "Advertisement" in compliance with DSA and UWG
Reviewed under the tobacco/nicotine advertising framework for any future regulatory changes
If Discover is purely algorithmic and based on user data, no advertising disclosure is required — but
document this clearly in your privacy policy (the recommendation algorithm uses personal data,
which requires disclosure under Article 13 GDPR).
